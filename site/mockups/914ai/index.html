The Problem

Booking the trip is the easy part.
Keeping it together is the hard part.

One trip, six emails. The Lufthansa confirmation in one inbox. The hotel from a different sender. The car transfer from a third. The dinner reservation you made yourself. The gate change that arrives at 2 a.m. local time.


Half the itinerary lives in your inbox, half on your calendar, half in your head. And the minute something changes, it's all scattered again.

The Solution

One place where the whole trip lives.

The assistant reads your mail, pulls the pieces together, and assembles the trip — automatically, as confirmations arrive. A tablet on your desk shows a nice photo when there's nothing to look at, and the whole trip on one clean page when there is. You still use Apple Calendar and your iPhone, just like always. Nothing new to learn.

On your desk · when there's news

When something new comes up, it shows you a clean list.

Tap the green check to add it to your calendar. Tap the red × to throw it away. That's the whole interaction. The microphone is right there if you want to ask it something instead.

Talk to it · it knows you

Ask the way you'd ask a person.

You said
"When am I going to Capri again?"
Assistant
Tuesday May 12. Lufthansa 458 from SFO at quarter to seven, connecting through Frankfurt. You're at Villa Brunella through Saturday.
You said
"Move the Gelsomina dinner to Friday."
Assistant
Moved. Da Gelsomina, Friday May 15 at eight. I'll let them know.

You don't need to remember commands. It already knows the places you go, the flights you take, and the people you're meeting. When you say "Capri," it knows you mean Villa Brunella. When you say "the Frankfurt connection," it knows which flight. Ask it anything about a trip and get an answer — not a list of emails to go dig through.

Quiet, by default

It only texts you when it matters.

The gate change at 2 a.m.? Yes, that's worth a ping. A promotional "deal on your next trip"? No. The assistant is tuned for signal, not noise — and if it ever gets it wrong, you tell it once and it learns. When you're not at your desk, it texts you the things that are actually actionable. Reply like a person — it understands.

What it won't bother you with: marketing emails, your own sent confirmations, newsletters, generic receipts, or anything you've told it to ignore.
It works the other way too. Something show up in WhatsApp, an airline app, or a travel agent's portal? Screenshot it and text the picture to the assistant — it reads the image and adds it to the trip, just like an email.
